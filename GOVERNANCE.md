# Governance

Collaborative Keystone is intended to be durable, auditable, and open by design.

This document defines the initial governance model for version 1.

---

## Project Origin

Collaborative Keystone was originated by River Madrid.

The long-term goal is for the project to survive beyond any one individual, but its origin should remain permanently documented in the repository history, governance documents, and public project materials.

---

## 1. Purpose

The purpose of governance in Collaborative Keystone is to:

- protect the integrity of the process
- keep the rules legible
- prevent silent or arbitrary power
- preserve public trust
- make major actions auditable

Governance exists to support the mechanism, not dominate it.

---

## 2. Version 1 Governance Model

Version 1 uses a founder-led stewardship model with documented moderator powers and audit requirements.

That means:

- the founder and early maintainers define the initial rules
- moderators enforce those rules within documented limits
- all major moderation actions are logged
- future versions may expand governance through dedicated platform and rules boards

Version 1 does **not** yet treat governance as fully community-controlled.

That expansion is planned for later iterations.

---

## 3. Scope of Moderator Authority

Moderators may only act within defined system states and rules.

Moderators may:

- archive a submission
- unarchive a submission
- freeze a submission pending review
- merge a submission into another after review
- review and resolve archive appeals
- attach moderator notes
- reverse prior moderator actions when justified

Moderators may **not** silently erase ordinary submissions from history.

---

## 4. Archive-Not-Delete Principle

Version 1 uses archive rather than ordinary deletion.

Archived proposals:

- remain viewable
- remain historically auditable
- remain eligible for appeal
- may become eligible for return through unarchive logic

The archive is not intended to function as trash.
It is a historical and governance-preserving state.

---

## 5. When a Proposal May Be Archived

A proposal may be archived when it is determined to be:

- materially unsafe
- illegal in use or prescription
- deceptive or fraudulent
- clear spam or abuse
- incomprehensible below minimum submission quality
- clearly not a fit for the board or locale
- superseded by a valid merge decision

Moderators should not archive merely because they personally dislike a proposal.

---

## 6. Unarchive Rules

A proposal may be unarchived when:

- the archive reason is no longer valid
- a distinction note shows the proposal is not actually a duplicate
- the proposal now meets minimum quality requirements
- the original moderation action was mistaken
- an appeal is upheld

---

## 7. Appeal Process

Version 1 appeal process:

1. the author submits an appeal
2. the appeal includes a short reason
3. the appeal enters moderator review
4. where possible, a different moderator from the original actor reviews it
5. the outcome is logged

Appeals exist to correct mistakes, not to create endless procedural loops.

---

## 8. Merge Governance

Merge votes from users are signals, not final authority.

A merge decision must be reviewable and logged.

When a proposal enters merge review:

- related proposals may be displayed together
- the author may submit a distinction note
- moderators review the relationship
- if a merge occurs, the merge is logged with reason and references

Version 1 does **not** auto-merge proposals.

---

## 9. Distinction Notes

When a proposal is under merge review, the author may submit a distinction note.

A distinction note exists to explain why the proposal is materially different from a related proposal.

Distinction notes should be visible:

- to moderators during review
- on the relationship between the two proposals
- to users viewing that merge relationship

This preserves nuance without rewriting history.

---

## 10. Proposal Editing

Version 1 should default to no user editing after submission.

If editing is later permitted:

- all edits must be logged
- prior versions must remain auditable
- material edits after voting begins must reset votes

This protects voters from having the substance of a proposal changed after votes have already been cast.

---

## 11. Live Voting Visibility

During an active cycle, public users should not see:

- live vote totals
- live support ratios
- live merge percentages
- live unsafe counts
- ranking math

This reduces bandwagoning, strategic suppression, and visible target selection.

Full results and relevant audit history may be published after cycle close.

---

## 12. Audit Trail Requirements

Major moderation actions must be logged.

This includes at minimum:

- archives
- unarchives
- merges
- freeze actions
- unfreeze actions
- appeal outcomes

Each audit record should store:

- action type
- proposal ID
- related proposal ID where applicable
- timestamp
- moderator ID
- reason
- note text where applicable
- snapshot references where needed

---

## 13. Maintainers

Early maintainers are expected to:

- preserve the public mission of the project
- document major changes
- avoid centralizing hidden power
- keep the system reproducible
- separate the engine from the brand where possible
- prepare the project to survive beyond any one individual

---

## 14. Future Governance Expansion

Later versions may introduce:

- Platform Improvement Board
- Governance / Rules Board
- trusted participation tiers for governance changes
- higher-friction approval thresholds for core rule changes

These expansions should be introduced carefully.

The same mechanism that governs issues and solutions should not be allowed to instantly destabilize the platform itself.

---

## 15. Guiding Governance Principles

- transparency over hidden power
- archive over erasure
- appeals over silence
- auditability over ambiguity
- practical stewardship over performative neutrality
- stability before expansion
